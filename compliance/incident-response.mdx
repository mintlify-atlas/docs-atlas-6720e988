A cybersecurity incident is any event that compromises the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of your organization's information assets — whether caused by an external attacker, a malicious insider, or an unintentional error. Ransomware encryption, unauthorized data exfiltration, account takeover, and system sabotage all qualify. The distinguishing characteristic of most incidents is not their technical sophistication but the gap between when they occur and when the organization discovers them. By then, the window for effective containment has often closed. ByteWise helps organizations reduce that gap through preparation, active testing, and expert response support.

<Warning>
  Most organizations do not detect an attack until after the damage is done. Attackers dwell in compromised environments for weeks or months before triggering visible harm — exfiltrating data, mapping systems, and establishing persistence while going undetected. Preparation is the only reliable counter.
</Warning>

## Questions to ask yourself now

Before an incident occurs, honest answers to these questions reveal your actual readiness:

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Would you know what was taken?" defaultOpen={true}>
    If an attacker exfiltrated data from your systems tonight, could you identify what was accessed, which records were copied, and who the affected individuals or clients are? Without a data inventory, access logs, and endpoint visibility, you cannot answer this question — and you cannot meet breach notification obligations that may apply under the LFPDPPP or contract terms.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Would you have evidence to act?" defaultOpen={true}>
    Legal, insurance, and regulatory responses to a security incident require forensic evidence — not assumptions. If your systems do not generate and retain sufficient logs, if timestamps are unreliable, or if affected systems have already been wiped or restored, you lose the ability to establish what happened, when, and how. Evidence that does not exist cannot be collected after the fact.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Would you discover the problem before it's too late?" defaultOpen={true}>
    Many organizations learn about a breach from an external source — a client, a regulatory notice, or a public disclosure by the attacker — rather than from their own monitoring. If you have no detection capability, no one is watching. The question is not whether an incident will occur, but whether you will find out in time to limit the impact.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## The 4 phases of incident response

Effective incident response follows a defined lifecycle. Organizations that improvise under pressure make costly mistakes — destroying evidence, missing containment steps, or prematurely declaring recovery while the threat persists.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Detection and triage">
    The incident is identified — through an alert, an anomaly detected by monitoring tools, a report from a user, or notification from an external party. The immediate priority is triage: confirming that an incident has occurred, classifying its severity, and activating the appropriate response team. Speed matters here — delayed detection extends the attacker's window of access.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Containment">
    Once the incident is confirmed, the goal is to stop it from spreading. Containment actions depend on the incident type and may include isolating affected systems from the network, revoking compromised credentials, blocking malicious IP addresses, or suspending services. Short-term containment prioritizes stopping the bleeding; long-term containment involves stabilizing the environment before eradication begins.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Eradication and recovery">
    After containment, the threat must be removed — malware deleted, unauthorized access revoked, vulnerabilities patched. Systems are then restored from clean backups or rebuilt from known-good states. Recovery must be validated before systems are returned to production: restoring an infected backup resets the clock rather than closing the incident.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Post-incident analysis">
    When the incident is closed, a structured review extracts lessons. What was the root cause? What controls failed or were absent? How long did the attacker have access? What would have reduced the impact? The post-incident report informs updates to your incident response plan, security controls, and monitoring configuration — converting a costly event into an improvement opportunity.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Building operational continuity

Incident response capability is inseparable from business continuity planning. The questions an incident forces you to answer — can you keep operating, can you recover your data, how long will recovery take — should be answered before the incident, not during it.

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Can you keep operating?" icon="building">
    Would your administrative functions continue normally if your systems went offline? Are your critical files, applications, and communications available from a secondary location? Organizations that depend entirely on a single environment with no fallback stop operating the moment that environment is compromised.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Can you recover your data?" icon="database">
    Do you have a documented backup and recovery process with tested recovery procedures? How long would restoration take — hours, days, weeks? A backup that has never been tested is an assumption, not a guarantee. Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) need to be defined and validated before they are needed.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Are you actually protected?" icon="shield">
    Many organizations believe they are protected until they face a real incident. Controls that exist on paper but are not tested, monitored, or maintained degrade over time. ByteWise's periodic testing and audit services verify that your defenses remain effective — not just documented.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

A plan that exists before an incident dramatically reduces response time and decision-making errors under pressure.
</Tip>
