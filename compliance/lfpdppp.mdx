The Ley Federal de Protección de Datos Personales en Posesión de los Particulares (LFPDPPP) is Mexico's federal law governing how private-sector organizations collect, use, store, transfer, and dispose of personal data. In force since 2010 and enforced by the Instituto Nacional de Transparencia, Acceso a la Información y Protección de Datos Personales (INAI), the law applies to any private individual or company that handles personal data — regardless of size or industry. Non-compliance exposes your organization to economic sanctions, regulatory proceedings, reputational damage, and loss of customer trust. ByteWise helps organizations assess their current compliance posture and implement the controls required to meet LFPDPPP obligations.

<Warning>
  INAI can impose fines ranging from 100 to 320,000 UMAs per infraction under the LFPDPPP. For organizations handling sensitive personal data — such as financial, health, or biometric information — sanctions can be substantially higher. Reputational consequences often outweigh the financial penalties.
</Warning>

## Key obligations under LFPDPPP

The LFPDPPP establishes a framework of obligations that apply throughout the personal data lifecycle.

**Privacy notice (Aviso de Privacidad)**
Every organization that collects personal data must provide a privacy notice at or before the point of collection. The notice must identify the data controller, describe the purposes for which data is processed, disclose whether data is transferred to third parties, and explain how data subjects can exercise their rights.

**Data subject rights (Derechos ARCO)**
Data subjects hold four enforceable rights: Access (to know what data is held), Rectification (to correct inaccurate data), Cancellation (to request deletion), and Opposition (to object to specific processing). Organizations must designate a responsible person or department and respond to ARCO requests within 20 business days.

**Security measures**
Organizations must implement administrative, technical, and physical security measures proportional to the sensitivity of the data processed. The law does not prescribe specific controls, but INAI guidance and sector regulations provide benchmarks. Failure to implement adequate security is an independent basis for sanction even without a breach.

**Breach notification**
When a security incident affects personal data in a way that could significantly harm data subjects, the organization must notify affected individuals promptly. INAI can require formal reporting depending on the severity and scope of the incident.

**Data transfers**
Transfers of personal data to third parties require either express consent from the data subject or a lawful basis under the LFPDPPP. International transfers carry additional requirements, including ensuring the recipient country provides adequate protection or obtaining contractual safeguards.

## Common compliance gaps ByteWise identifies

Most organizations that have not undergone a formal LFPDPPP assessment share predictable gaps:

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Missing or deficient privacy notices">
    Many organizations collect personal data through websites, intake forms, or employee records without a compliant privacy notice — or with a notice that omits required elements such as transfer disclosures or ARCO contact information.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="No documented ARCO rights procedure">
    Without a formal process for receiving, validating, and responding to ARCO requests within the legal timeframe, organizations are exposed to complaints filed directly with INAI.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Undocumented data processing inventory">
    Organizations frequently lack a complete record of what personal data they hold, where it is stored, who has access, and for how long it is retained. This makes it impossible to respond accurately to data subject requests or assess breach scope.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Inadequate third-party contracts">
    Vendors, service providers, and processors who handle personal data on the organization's behalf must be governed by contracts that assign privacy responsibilities. Many organizations have no such clauses in their supplier agreements.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="No security measures for sensitive data">
    Sensitive personal data — including health records, biometric data, financial details, and political or religious affiliations — requires heightened security controls. Organizations often apply the same (often insufficient) controls to all data categories.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
